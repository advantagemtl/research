CHAPTER 1: Introduction to Google Ads

Key Principle

Smart businesses fish where the fish are.

Google Ads allows you to remarket to customers, turning a single website visit into multiple opportunities to build your brand.

What Google Ads Can Do for Your Business

Google Ads enables you to:

  1. Place your company, product, or service at the top of Google search results at the exact moment customers search for relevant keywords—when they’re ready to buy. This includes:
    • Standard Search Ads
    • Shopping Ads (product images + text)
    • Local Service Ads (LSAs) for local businesses
  2. Reach customers on thousands of websites and blogs via the Google Display Network (GDN) as they browse the web.
  3. Follow customers around the internet with remarketing, showing your ads on Google, YouTube, and GDN partner sites.
  4. Advertise on YouTube (the world’s largest video platform) and Gmail (the world’s largest free email service).
  5. Promote your mobile app through in-app advertising.

The Networks That Make Up Google Ads

  • Google Search – Google.com, the world’s largest search engine
  • Shopping Ads – Product showcases with images and text
  • Local Service Ads (LSAs) – For local businesses
  • YouTube – Text and video ads on the largest video platform
  • Gmail – Ads in the largest free email service
  • Google App – Ads in one of the most popular mobile apps
  • Google Play – App marketplace ads and in-app advertising
  • Google Display Network (GDN) – Third-party websites, blogs, and apps that display Google-served ads (distinct from Google.com)

Important Distinction:

  • Search intent: Customers actively searching on Google.com → usually highest ROI
  • Browse intent: Customers passively browsing websites or watching YouTube → lower intent, often lower ROI

Most advertisers get the best results focusing on active searchers on Google.com rather than passive browsers on the GDN or YouTube.

Major Drawbacks and Conflicts of Interest

Google Ads has significant challenges:

  • Confusing terminology and frequently changing user interface
  • Built-in conflict of interest: Google earns money from clicks, while you earn money from conversions (sales, leads, etc.)
  • Pay-per-click (PPC) model: You only pay when someone clicks, but clicks ≠ conversions
  • Google uses misleading language, hides features, and pushes AI tools to encourage higher click volume and spending
  • “Support” from Google employees often prioritizes Google’s revenue over your ROI

Core Contradiction:
Google optimizes for clicks.
You need conversions.
These goals are not aligned—you cannot fully trust Google’s recommendations.

How the Google Ads Auction Works

Google Ads is primarily a pay-per-click (PPC) auction:

  • Advertisers bid against each other for clicks
  • You pay only when a user clicks your ad

Ranking on the SERP (Search Engine Results Page) depends on three main factors:

  1. Your bid (how much you’re willing to pay per click)
  2. Ad quality/relevance (Quality Score)
  3. Ad extensions

Google claims a higher Quality Score (better relevance to the search query) lowers your cost-per-click (CPC) and improves position—even allowing lower bids to outrank higher ones.

Catch: Quality Score rewards ads that generate more clicks for Google, not necessarily those that drive conversions for you.

Recent and Ongoing Trends

  • Keyword match types (exact, phrase) no longer behave as advertised
  • Heavy push toward AI-driven ad creation, targeting, and bidding—often increasing spend while reducing advertiser control
  • Nothing in Google Ads is as straightforward as it appears

Ad Structure

A typical Google Search Ad includes:

  • URL – Clickable website address
  • Headline – Up to three clickable parts
  • Description – Non-clickable supporting text
  • Extensions – Additional elements (site links, call buttons, location, etc.) that can appear below the ad

Tools for Competitor Research

  • SpyFu (paid): https://jmlinks.com/69h
  • Google Ad Transparency Center (free): https://jmlinks.com/69j

Smart Mode vs. Expert Mode

  • Smart Mode → Google takes excessive control, prioritizes clicks and higher spend → avoid it
  • Expert Mode → Gives you full control and oversight of AI features → recommended

To switch: Click the Settings gear → “Switch to Expert Mode”
Google Help: https://jmlinks.com/69k

Final Advice

  • Be open to differing opinions—listening to contrary advice is a valuable life skill.
  • Stay alert for gotchas (hidden pitfalls) throughout Google Ads.
  • The interface changes frequently; you may need to hunt for features mentioned in guides.

CHAPTER 2: Google Ads Gotchas

Google Ads is full of hidden “gotchas” — misleading defaults and features that can waste your budget by targeting the wrong keywords, running ads on low-quality sites, overpaying for clicks, and driving traffic to ineffective pages.

Gotcha #1: Incoherent Website or Landing Page

Before spending money on ads, ensure your website clearly communicates your offer and converts visitors.

Key requirements for a landing page:

  • Instantly explain your value proposition (what you sell and why it’s better)
  • Build trust and authority (reviews, clear policies, professional design)
  • Offer a clear, low-pressure next step (free consultation, ebook, easy checkout)

Analogy: Don’t ask for marriage on the first date — offer something free and non-threatening first.

Pro Tip: Search Google for your own keywords, click competitor ads, and evaluate their landing pages with a “guppy brain” (simple, overwhelmed customer):

  1. Does it quickly explain the offer?
  2. Does it feel trustworthy?
  3. Is the next step obvious and easy?

Clicks cost money — never send paid traffic to a confusing, slow, or untrustworthy page.

Gotcha #2: Bad Keyword Match Types & Missing Negatives

Google has loosened keyword matching, favoring broader (and more expensive) triggers that benefit Google’s revenue.

Always use proper match types:

  • Phrase match: "motorcycle insurance"
  • Exact match: [motorcycle insurance]

Without quotes or brackets, Google may show your ad for irrelevant variations (e.g., “free cat boarding” when you sell luxury boarding).

Add negative keywords like -free, -cheap, -discount to block low-intent searches.

Rule: Specify, don’t trust Google’s defaults.

Gotcha #3: Artificial Intelligence Overreach

Google pushes AI features that prioritize clicks over conversions.

  • Recommendations & Optimization Score → Often overly optimistic; most increase spend
  • Smart Campaigns / Performance Max → Poor targeting and quality; avoid for serious advertisers
  • Local Service Ads (LSAs) → Limited control; monitor closely if used
  • Keyword Suggestions → Usually junk that explodes impressions/clicks
  • Smart/Automatic Bidding → Can overbid; beginners should use Manual CPC
  • Ad Suggestions → Opt out (Account → Settings → Ad suggestions → Don’t auto-apply)
  • Automated Assets → Turn off (Admin → Account settings → Auto-apply settings)

Google AI is not aligned with your conversion goals.

Gotcha #4: Google Display Network (GDN)

Google Ads runs on two very different networks:

  • Search Network (Google.com + some partners) → High intent
  • Display Network (YouTube videos, Gmail, blogs, low-quality sites) → Low intent, often fraudulent clicks

Default: Both networks ON → Most advertisers should turn GDN OFF.

When creating a campaign (Website Traffic or Leads), uncheck “Display Network”. Also consider unchecking “Search Partners”.

Only enable GDN after understanding and testing it.

Gotcha #5: Conversions Across Devices (Especially Mobile)

Default: Ads run on desktops, tablets, and mobile phones.

Many sites aren’t mobile-friendly → high bounce rates, low conversions from mobile clicks.

Check performance:

  • In Google Ads → Campaigns/Ad Groups → Devices
  • In Google Analytics (jmlinks.com/69q) → Segments → Compare Mobile vs. Desktop/Tablets

Gut check: Browse your own site on a phone. Can a visitor easily convert?

If not, reduce mobile bid adjustment (e.g., -50% or -100%) or turn mobile off.

Always optimize for mobile experience.

Gotcha #6: Ignoring Alternatives to Google Ads

Before (or while) using Google Ads, evaluate higher-ROI options:

  • SEO (Search Engine Optimization)
  • Email marketing
  • Influencer / PR
  • Review marketing
  • Platform-specific ads (Amazon, eBay, etc.)
  • Traditional advertising
  • Word-of-mouth / eWOM (often the highest ROI)

To-Do: Allocate budget across multiple channels for best overall ROI.

CHAPTER 3: Keywords

Keywords are the foundation of Google Ads success. Choose the right ones → profitable campaigns. Choose poorly → wasted budget.

Core Principle: Focus on transactional keywords where users are ready to buy.

Types of Keywords

  • Educational / Early-Stage: User is researching or learning.
    Examples: “Siamese cats”, “how to cut a cat’s claws”, “where to buy a cat”
  • Transactional / Late-Stage: User is in “buy mode”.
    Examples: “cat boarding”, “cat boarding in San Francisco”, “cat grooming service”
  • Micro Keywords: Highly specific, low-volume but precise intent.
    Examples: “luxury cat boarding”, “cat microchipping”, “diamond cat collar”
  • Short-Tail vs. Long-Tail: Short = few words (e.g., “cat boarding”). Long = more words (e.g., “cheap cat boarding in the Castro district San Francisco”).
    Tip: Focus on intent (transactional vs. educational) rather than word count.
  • Branded / Navigational: Your own company name (e.g., “Jason’s Cat Emporium”).
    Bid on your brand to control the SERP and block competitors.
  • Reputational: Brand + “reviews” (e.g., “Jason’s Cat Emporium reviews”)
  • Competitor Brands: Aggressive strategy — bid on competitor names (careful with trademarks in ad text).
  • Negative Keywords: Exclude unwanted traffic.
    Examples: -free, -cheap (for premium services)
  • Ambiguous Keywords: Could include non-target customers.
    Examples: “pet boarding” (includes dogs), “animal boarding”

Priority: Transactional keywords drive revenue. Educational keywords are usually lower value.

Volume vs. Value

Use the “fish in a pond” analogy:

  • Keyword Type = Species of fish (salmon vs. tilapia)
  • Volume = Number of fish (search volume per month — check in Google Ads Keyword Planner)
  • Value = Price per pound (higher CPC usually signals higher commercial intent and profitability)

Example: “cat collars” may have high volume but low value. “luxury cat boarding San Francisco” has low volume but high value.

Rule: Riches are in the niches — prioritize high-value niche keywords.

Keyword Brainstorming Process

Gather your team and systematically brainstorm:

  • What exact words would a ready-to-buy customer type into Google?
  • Which keywords clearly indicate buying intent?
  • Which are early-stage (educational) vs. late-stage (transactional)?
  • How do keywords differ across customer segments or product lines?
  • Which helper words signal non-buyers? (e.g., “free”, “cheap” → negative keywords)
  • Capture synonyms and related terms (e.g., lawyer vs. attorney, boarding vs. hotel, cat vs. kitten)
  • Include broader umbrella terms (e.g., “pet boarding”, “law firm”)

Document everything in a Keyword Worksheet.

Reverse-Engineering Competitor Keywords

Three effective methods:

  1. Source Code Inspection
    Visit competitor site → Ctrl+U (view page source) → Look for:
    <title>, <meta name="description">, <meta name="keywords">
  2. Read Competitor Ads
    Perform your brainstormed searches on Google → Read top ads out loud → Note synonyms and phrasing
  3. Spy Tools
    Use SpyFu: https://jmlinks.com/69s
    Enter a keyword → See who advertises, their ad copy, and estimated keywords

Tools Mentioned

  • Google Ads Keyword Planner (Tools → Planning → Keyword Planner) → For volume and CPC estimates
  • SpyFu → Competitor keyword and ad research: https://jmlinks.com/69s
